The Cruachan Equinox

The premise:

The ancient Irish afforded special significance to locations where they could observe, on specific divisional days of the year, the sun rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains.

Specifically in the Cruachan context:

Rathcroghan Mound1 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun rise from Corn Hill2.

Rathra Ceremonial Enclosure3 has been positioned to observe the equinox sun set into Croagh Patrick4.

Locations

Rathcroghan Mound1

Rathcroghan is identified as the site of Cruachan, the traditional capital of the Connachta, the prehistoric and early historic rulers of the western territory.

The focal point of the complex, Rathcroghan mound is a broad, flat-topped circular mound with an average diameter of 89 m at the base and a height of 5.5 m. Surveying has revealed that Rathcroghan mound was built on top of an existing monument that was made of two concentric stone built ring banks. A huge enclosure measuring 360 m in diameter and 5 m in depth was also discovered during surveying. This enclosure surrounds the great mound and various other monuments, facilitating comparisons with other royal sites in Ireland such as Tara, Emain Macha and DÃºn Ailinne, which have similar enclosures.

Cruachan features prominently in the mythological Ulster Cycle and it was the home of one of its chief characters Queen Medb.

Locations

Corn Hill2

Corn Hill, also called Carn Hill or Carn Clonhugh (Irish: Carn Clainne Aodha or Sliabh CairbrÃ©), is a hill in County Longford, Republic of Ireland. At 278 metres above sea level, it is the highest hill in the county. The hill is particularly important for the two possible passage grave structures atop it. The largest example is Cairn A (or Carn Caille), marking the highest physical point of the hill with a diameter of 18 m and height of 3 m.

Cairn A (or Carn Caille) has a legend which associates the location as the burial place of Queen Medb's nephew and murderer, Furbaide Ferbend. A second legend attributes its creation by the Cailleach a Bheara or ancient Hag/Witch who dropped stones out of her apron as she flew over the hill. This legend is also associated with many Passage grave sites throughout the island of Ireland.

The Sheegeeragh Passage tomb (approx 8 Kilometers sw from Rathcroghan mound) has a passage which is directed at Corn Hill.

Locations

Rathra3

Rathra is a multivallate ceremonial enclosure, near Castlerea, County Roscommon, Ireland. This is one of only a handful of quadri-vallate (four banks) enclosures in the whole of Ireland and probably the most spectacular. The Rathra Enclosure lies near the archaeological monuments known as the Rathcroghan Complex and this enclosure is sometimes considered to be part of this group.

The enclosure contains a bowl barrow and a mound barrow which originally had a standing stone on it's summit.

The site sits just off the shoulder of Mewlaghadooey Hill, the summit of which is c. 700m to the ESE and is occupied by a hillfort containing a mound which may be a passage tomb.

Croagh Patrick4

Croagh Patrick (Irish: Cruach PhÃ¡draig, meaning "(Saint) Patrick's Stack") and nicknamed the Reek, is a 764 m mountain and an important site of pilgrimage in Mayo, Ireland.

On the last Sunday in July, thousands of pilgrims climb Croagh Patrick in honour of one of Irelands patron saints - Saint Patrick. Some pilgrims carry out 'rounding rituals', in which they pray while walking sunwise around features on the mountain. This activity is regarded by many as a continuation of the pagan Lughnasa festival, the start of the harvest season. St. Patrick's day is celebrated at the spring equinox.

The mountain may have held a pre-christian association with Crom Cruach, a pagan deity.

Developing the hypothesis
Rathcroghan1 - Corn Hill2
Step 1
Establish potential

To establish if there is any potential for an equinox alignment between these sites we first calculate the Just below horizon equinox sunrise azimuth for Rathcroghan. We plot the azimuth as an orthodrome line overlain onto a spherical mercator projection of the earth. Or, more simply put, we plot the line towards the equinox sun as it rises on the horizon.

This is very simply achieved using  MACE.

MACE (a Map-based Archaeoastronomy Calculator and Explorer) calculates and projects: Solar - Lunar, Rise - Set, Azimuths for Solstices, Cross Quarters, Equinoxs and Standstills, for any northern hemisphere location, and any time period.

Step 2
Determine site to site visibility

To confirm Corn Hill is visible from Rathcroghan mound we create what is termed a viewshed. A viewshed is the geographical area that is visible from a location. A viewshed is calculated using a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) of the earths surface. The colourised portion of the map represents those areas visible from Rathcroghan. The furthest visible point through 360Â° (the viewshed horizon) is indicated by the darker coloured line.

Viewshed calculation is a feature common to most GIS (geographic information system) software. The easiest and most accessible method of creating a viewshed is via the  www.heywhatsthat.com website.

Step 3
Assess Potential

To assess if there is any possibility for an alignment we can zoom to the Corn hill location and observe that Corn Hill is visible from Rathcroghan mound. We can also see that the projected equinox sunrise line crosses through Corn Hill. We can reasonably conclude that there is a distinct possibility of an alignment.

Step 4
Modelling

Again using data derived from a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) of the earths surface, we can create a valid horizon line for the Rathcroghan mound location. We accurately position Corn Hill on the horizon. A simulation is then created using the Stellarium planetarium programme.

The computer model of the Rathcroghan equinox sunrise shows the very first rays of the sun become visible at the highest point on Corn Hill, i.e. the Carn Caille passage tomb.
